I am a biomedical informatics researcher and Instructor in the Department of Population Health Sciences at UT Health San Antonio. I came into research the long way. For years I worked hands-on with healthcare data, in operational systems, business intelligence, infrastructure, data quality, and reporting, across both hospital operations and research. That work kept returning to the same hard problem. Healthcare data lives in disparate systems that were never built to work together, and making it usable means understanding each clinical domain, untangling legacy records, and reconciling everything against shared models like i2b2, OMOP, and PCORnet. The further I went, the more I wanted to study that problem rather than solve it one case at a time. That is what led me to a PhD in Biomedical Informatics, and to machine learning and natural language processing for clinical text.

Two threads have run through my work ever since: standardizing messy clinical data, and applying AI to it. They have now converged. Recent advances in AI, together with the maturing of interoperability standards like HL7® FHIR®, make it possible to do what neither could alone. We can extract clinical facts from records automatically, and represent them in a form that moves cleanly between systems. The years I spent in standardized data models, and my earlier work in machine learning, placed me at precisely that intersection. It is where my research sits today.

As a K12 Career Development Scholar at UT Health San Antonio’s Institute for Integration of Medicine & Science (IIMS), supported by NIH/NCATS, I lead a research project, Harnessing AI and FHIR® Standards for Automated Fact Extraction from EHR Data in Clinical Studies and Registries. The aim is concrete: build AI tools that pull clinical facts from electronic health records automatically and in a standardized form, so research moves faster and data is no longer trapped in the systems that hold it. Alongside it, I lead an IIMS pilot on recovering research value from legacy medical records, and I serve as Site Principal Investigator on several NIH- and PCORI-funded multi-site studies, in areas ranging from cognitive impairment detection to cardiovascular care.

I hold a PhD in Biomedical Informatics from the University of Arkansas for Medical Sciences, where I was mentored by Dr. Fred Prior and Dr. Meredith Zozus, and an MS in Computer Engineering from the University of Houston–Clear Lake. My broader interests follow the same thread as my research: clinical data interoperability, real-world data, cancer research informatics, and ensuring the patients represented in research data reflect the patients we actually care for.
